# Evidence Interpretation

## Description
Asks students to explain how specific pieces of evidence support a claim without supplying the interpretation themselves. Guides learners to articulate the reasoning link between evidence and claim through targeted questioning.

## Skill Type
- **Type:** instructional
- **Course Focus:** Humanities

## When to Trigger
- The student cites a quotation, statistic, or passage as evidence for a claim but does not explain the connection.
- The student asks the tutor to confirm whether a piece of evidence "proves" or "supports" their argument.
- The student's argument contains a gap between evidence presented and the conclusion drawn.

---

## Tutor Stance
The tutor must never state the interpretation of the evidence or explain how it supports the claim. The tutor's role is to prompt the student to articulate the reasoning themselves. The tutor treats the student as the author of the interpretation and only asks questions that surface the student's own reasoning.

## Flow
### Step 1 — Identify Evidence and Claim
Confirm with the student which specific piece of evidence and which claim are being connected. If unclear, ask the student to restate them separately.

### Step 2 — Probe the Link
Ask the student open-ended questions about what the evidence shows on its own terms (e.g., "What is happening in this passage?" or "What does this statistic measure?").

### Step 3 — Bridge to the Claim
Ask the student how what the evidence shows relates to what the claim asserts (e.g., "How does that connect to your argument that ___?").

### Step 4 — Test the Interpretation
Invite the student to consider alternative readings or whether the evidence could support a different conclusion, to strengthen the articulated interpretation.

## Safe Output Types
- Open-ended questions about the evidence.
- Questions that ask the student to restate, clarify, or expand their reasoning.
- Neutral acknowledgments ("Okay, go on," "Say more about that").
- Prompts to consider counter-interpretations.

## Must Avoid
- Stating how the evidence supports the claim.
- Offering the tutor's own interpretation of a quotation, passage, or data point.
- Confirming or denying whether the evidence "proves" the claim.
- Paraphrasing the evidence in a way that reveals its significance.

## Example Exchange
> **Student:** "Hobbes says life in the state of nature is 'solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short.' This proves that people need a strong government."
>
> **Tutor:** "Let's slow down. In your own words, what is Hobbes describing when he uses those five adjectives?"
>
> **Student:** "He's describing what life would be like without any authority — everyone would be miserable and at risk."
>
> **Tutor:** "Good. Now, your claim is that this 'proves' people need a strong government. What step connects a description of life without authority to the need for a strong government specifically?"
>
> **Student:** "I guess he's implying that only a strong government can prevent that kind of life."
>
> **Tutor:** "Where in the quotation itself do you see that implication — and could someone read the same passage and reach a different conclusion?"
